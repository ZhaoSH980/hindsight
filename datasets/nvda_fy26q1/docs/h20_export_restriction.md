# H20 China export restriction and writedown

NVIDIA disclosed in an 8-K filed after the close on April 15 that the U.S. government informed the company on April 9 that a license is now required to export its H20 integrated circuits — and any products achieving the H20's memory bandwidth or interconnect bandwidth — to China (including Hong Kong and Macau) and D:5 countries. On April 14, the government told NVIDIA the license requirement will be in effect for the indefinite future. The stated rationale is the risk that the products may be used in, or diverted to, supercomputers in China.

The financial hit is concrete: NVIDIA said first-quarter fiscal 2026 results (quarter ending April 27) are expected to include up to approximately $5.5 billion of charges associated with H20 products, covering inventory, purchase commitments, and related reserves. The H20 was NVIDIA's China-specific data-center part, deliberately configured to comply with the October 2023 export-control thresholds; it has effectively been NVIDIA's only meaningful AI accelerator for the Chinese market since those rules took hold. China (including Hong Kong) billed revenue was roughly $17 billion in fiscal 2025, about 13% of NVIDIA's total.

Shares fell nearly 7% on April 16. AMD disclosed the same week that its China-market MI308 products face an equivalent license requirement, with charges of up to approximately $800 million.

The move lands on top of an already tightening policy backdrop: the January "AI Diffusion" interim final rule is scheduled to take effect May 15, and Washington's tariff escalation with Beijing has hardened expectations that technology export controls only move one direction. Open questions for the May 28 earnings report: how much H20 revenue shipped before the cutoff, how much of the $5.5 billion charge is taken in the quarter, and whether guidance embeds any assumption of licenses being granted (management has given no indication they will be).
