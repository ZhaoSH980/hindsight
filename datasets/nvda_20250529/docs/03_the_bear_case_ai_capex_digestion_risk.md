# The bear case: AI capex digestion risk

The bull case for NVIDIA needs only one assumption: that hyperscaler capital spending keeps compounding. The bear case is that we are watching the top of an investment cycle, and the warning lights are already blinking.

Start with the lease checks. TD Cowen's supply-chain work found Microsoft walked away from data-center projects totaling roughly two gigawatts of capacity across the U.S. and Europe over six months — driven, in their read, by a decision not to support incremental OpenAI training workloads. Microsoft disputes the interpretation and reiterated its roughly $80 billion spending plan, and Google and Meta appear to have backfilled some of the capacity. But leases are the earliest-moving indicator of hyperscaler intent, and they moved down. By late April, Wells Fargo's checks suggested Amazon Web Services had paused a portion of its own lease negotiations — a characterization AWS disputed, but the second such headline in two months.

Second, efficiency. DeepSeek's January release demonstrated near-frontier model quality at a fraction of the assumed training cost. If algorithmic progress keeps cutting the compute required per unit of capability, the mapping from "AI demand" to "GPU units" weakens — the core of what Alibaba chairman Joe Tsai called a nascent bubble in U.S. data-center construction when he spoke in March.

Third, the accounting. Massed GPU fleets are depreciating assets on five-to-six-year schedules against still-thin AI application revenue. Every quarter of elevated capex raises the future earnings hurdle for the buyers — a treadmill that ends either with AI monetization arriving on time, or with a capex digestion phase like 2019's cloud pause.

Add the idiosyncratic risks: the H20 ban just removed most of NVIDIA's China data-center business, and the 10-K shows striking customer concentration, with individual direct customers repeatedly crossing 10% of revenue.

None of this says the May 28 print misses; the spring earnings round showed 2025 budgets intact. The bear case is about fiscal 2027 estimates, which embed continued hypergrowth. Cycles look permanent until the quarter they don't.
