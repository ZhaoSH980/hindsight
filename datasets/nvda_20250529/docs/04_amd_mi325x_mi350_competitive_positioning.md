# AMD MI325X/MI350 competitive positioning

AMD remains the only merchant-silicon challenger with a shipping big-memory AI accelerator, but the gap to NVIDIA is not closing quickly. The Instinct MI325X — 256GB of HBM3E, positioned against NVIDIA's H200 — entered production in the fourth quarter of 2024, with broad system availability from Dell, Lenovo, Supermicro, and others beginning in the first quarter of 2025. The pitch is inference price/performance: more memory per accelerator at a lower price point than comparable Hopper-generation parts.

The nearer-term swing factor is the MI350 series, built on the CDNA 4 architecture with 288GB of HBM3E and a claimed 35x generational uplift in inference performance versus CDNA 3. On its February earnings call AMD said the MI350 launch has been pulled forward to around the middle of 2025, sampling now with lead customers — placing it against NVIDIA's Blackwell rather than Hopper. The MI400 series follows in 2026, targeting rack-scale systems, the segment NVIDIA already occupies with GB200 NVL72.

The scale mismatch remains stark. AMD's entire data-center segment did $12.6 billion of revenue in calendar 2024 (up 94%), of which AI GPUs contributed more than $5 billion; NVIDIA's data-center business did $115.2 billion in its fiscal 2025. Deployments at Meta (Llama inference on MI300X), Microsoft Azure, and Oracle validate the hardware, but the ROCm software stack is still the chief adoption barrier cited by customers migrating CUDA-optimized workloads, and AMD has no answer yet to NVIDIA's NVLink-based rack-scale systems or networking attach.

For NVIDIA, the practical takeaway is that AMD constrains pricing at the margin in inference-heavy deals but is unlikely to cap the Blackwell ramp this year. Both stocks have sold off hard this week amid the tariff-driven market rout; the competitive story is unchanged by the macro.
