# Blackwell ramp supply-chain readout

At the GTC keynote in San Jose on March 18, Jensen Huang laid out an annual data-center product cadence and put numbers behind the Blackwell ramp. Huang said the top four U.S. cloud providers have ordered 3.6 million Blackwell GPUs in 2025, versus roughly 1.3 million Hopper GPUs bought in Hopper's peak year — a slide he used to argue that demand for AI compute is accelerating, not digesting. He repeated his forecast that global data-center buildouts will approach $1 trillion in annual capital spending by 2028.

On the roadmap: Blackwell Ultra (the GB300 NVL72 rack configuration) is slated for the second half of 2025, offering more memory and higher inference throughput for reasoning models. The next architecture, Vera Rubin, arrives in the second half of 2026 with a custom Vera CPU, followed by Rubin Ultra in 2027. NVIDIA also introduced Dynamo, an open-source inference-serving framework tuned for multi-node reasoning workloads, and previewed co-packaged silicon-photonics networking switches (Spectrum-X and Quantum-X) aimed at million-GPU AI factories.

The supply-chain picture supports a steepening ramp. NVIDIA disclosed $11.0 billion of Blackwell revenue in the January quarter — its fastest product ramp ever — after early GB200 NVL72 rack-integration and thermal issues reported in late 2024 slowed initial system builds. ODM partners and hyperscalers have since moved to volume rack shipments, and TSMC has been expanding CoWoS advanced-packaging capacity through 2025 to relieve the main bottleneck. The open question for the ramp is less demand than mix: rack-scale NVL72 systems carry more content per GPU but are harder to build, test, and deploy than HGX boards, and the GB300 transition later this year adds another changeover.

Shares have lagged the news flow: the stock remains well below its January high amid market-wide risk-off and China policy questions.
