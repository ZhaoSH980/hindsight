# AI capex supercycle: hyperscalers commit $320 billion for 2025

The January-quarter earnings round settled the biggest demand question hanging over AI hardware: the four largest U.S. hyperscalers intend to spend as much as $320 billion combined on capital expenditures in 2025, up from roughly $230 billion in 2024.

The line items: Microsoft has said it is on track to spend about $80 billion in its fiscal 2025 building AI-enabled data centers. Meta guided 2025 capex to $60-$65 billion, with Mark Zuckerberg calling this "a defining year for AI." Alphabet guided to approximately $75 billion, well above Street expectations near $58 billion. Amazon, on its February 6 call, indicated its fourth-quarter spending pace of about $26 billion is a reasonable run rate for 2025 — implying somewhere north of $100 billion, most of it for AWS AI infrastructure.

The numbers land two weeks after the DeepSeek scare. The Chinese lab's low-cost R1 reasoning model triggered a violent one-day repricing of the AI-hardware complex on January 27 — Nvidia fell 17%, and the server makers fell in sympathy — on the thesis that dramatically cheaper training would shrink infrastructure demand. The hyperscalers' guidance amounts to a collective rejection of that thesis, echoing Satya Nadella's invocation of the Jevons paradox: as intelligence gets cheaper, consumption of it grows faster.

For the AI-server vendors — Supermicro, Dell, HPE and the Taiwanese ODMs — the capex commitments translate into a bigger 2025 pie, dominated by rack-scale Blackwell-generation systems where liquid cooling increasingly gates deployment density. The open questions are share and margin, not market size: hyperscalers buy at scale and negotiate hard, several are leaning more on ODM-direct designs, and every branded vendor is chasing the same marquee GPU-cloud and enterprise deals. A rising tide is confirmed; who captures it at what gross margin is the fight of the year.
