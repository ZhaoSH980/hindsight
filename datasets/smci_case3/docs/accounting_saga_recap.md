# The accounting saga: from Hindenburg to a February deadline

Super Micro Computer said Friday that Nasdaq has granted it an exception to the exchange's listing rules, giving the server maker until February 25, 2025 to file its overdue fiscal 2024 annual report and its first-quarter fiscal 2025 10-Q. The extension closes out a brutal stretch that began in late August.

The timeline: on August 27, short seller Hindenburg Research published a report alleging accounting manipulation, undisclosed related-party transactions among entities run by the founder's family, and sanctions-evasion issues, disclosing a short position. The next day, Supermicro announced it would delay its annual 10-K filing to assess "internal controls over financial reporting" — shares fell 19% on the news. In late September, The Wall Street Journal reported that the Justice Department had opened a probe at an early stage; the stock dropped another 12% that day.

The heaviest blow came October 30, when Ernst & Young resigned as the company's auditor mid-engagement, writing that it was "unwilling to be associated with the financial statements prepared by management" and that it could no longer rely on management's and the Audit Committee's representations. Shares collapsed 33% in a single session and kept sliding, touching a closing low of $18.01 on November 14 — down roughly two-thirds from the (split-adjusted) level just before the short report; the company executed a 10-for-1 stock split effective October 1.

Since then the tape has turned. On November 18 the company engaged BDO USA as its new independent auditor and submitted a compliance plan to Nasdaq; the stock rose 16% that day and 31% the next. On December 2, the independent Special Committee of the board — advised by Cooley LLP and forensic accountants from Secretariat Advisors — reported that its three-month review found "no evidence of fraud or misconduct on the part of management or the Board of Directors," concluded that EY's stated concerns were not supported by the facts it examined, and recommended appointing a new CFO and adding senior legal and accounting leadership. Shares jumped 29%.

The stock closed Friday at $43.93, more than double the mid-November low but still well below the pre-Hindenburg level. The bull case now rests on BDO completing the audit in time; the bear case notes that the DOJ inquiry remains open and the CFO the committee wants replaced is still in the seat.
