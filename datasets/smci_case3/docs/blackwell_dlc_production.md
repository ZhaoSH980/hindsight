# Supermicro puts NVIDIA Blackwell rack-scale systems into full production

Supermicro announced full production availability of its end-to-end AI data center Building Block Solutions accelerated by the NVIDIA Blackwell platform, headlined by new systems built around the NVIDIA HGX B200 8-GPU board and rack-scale SuperClusters based on the NVIDIA GB200 NVL72.

The GB200 NVL72 SuperCluster packs 72 Blackwell GPUs and 36 Grace CPUs into a single liquid-cooled rack acting as one giant GPU, with 130 TB/s of aggregate GPU communication bandwidth over NVLink. Supermicro's pitch is that it can deliver these not as bare boxes but as fully integrated data-center building blocks: rack-level integration including network switching and cabling, cluster-level L12 validation, and turn-key global delivery with professional support.

The differentiator the company keeps hammering is cooling. Supermicro claims an end-to-end direct liquid-cooling (DLC) stack developed in-house — cold plates, coolant distribution units and manifolds, cooling towers — with new liquid-to-liquid (L2L) and liquid-to-air (L2A) configurations that let customers deploy dense Blackwell racks even in data centers without facility water. Management has argued for several quarters that DLC is moving from niche to mainstream as per-rack power for Blackwell-generation systems climbs beyond what air can economically remove, and that Supermicro's first-mover volume production in DLC is a structural advantage over larger rivals.

The Blackwell transition matters commercially because it resets the competitive board. The prior Hopper generation (H100/H200) saw Supermicro win outsized share of GPU server shipments on speed-to-market; whether that playbook repeats with GB200-class rack systems — where Dell, HPE and the Taiwanese ODMs are all competing aggressively for the largest AI-infrastructure deals — is the central volume question for calendar 2025. Full production availability announced today puts Supermicro among the first vendors shipping Blackwell rack-scale systems at volume.
